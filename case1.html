REGULATORY STRATEGY · RWA

The Greeks were fine. The market wasn't.

A risk framework built on Delta and Gamma alone was missing the most critical signal in the portfolio—how assets actually behave when the "unthinkable" happens.

3 min read
OUTCOME

Secured PRA compliance by bridging the gap between front-office reality and back-office models.

This project wasn't just a technical upgrade; it was a high-stakes reconciliation of three different worldviews: the Trader (who wants precision), the Regulator (who wants stability), and the System (which was never built to talk to both at once). The shift from Greek-based VaR to Full Reval is effectively the "Coming of Age" story for market risk infrastructure.

01. THE CONTEXT

"Close enough" is no longer a regulatory strategy.

In a "quiet" market, Taylor-series approximations (Greeks) are brilliant. They are fast, light, and "close enough." But the PRA and FRTB standards were born from the realization that "close enough" is how banks fail during a liquidity crunch. The business mandate was clear: The Risk Model must match the Trading Desk. If a Trader’s book shows a massive loss due to a barrier breach, the Risk Manager’s dashboard cannot show a minor dip just because a "Gamma approximation" smoothed out the curve. This isn't just a math error; it’s a capital misallocation.

02. THE PROBLEM / TECH DEBT

Performing open-heart surgery on a running engine

Our legacy engines were designed to be "Sensitivity Consumers." They were built to ingest a flat file of Greeks once a day and do basic multiplication.

The Conflict: To get to Full Reval, the engine had to stop being a passive consumer and start being an active participant.

The Constraint: We couldn't just throw away the legacy system—it was the "Source of Truth" for reporting. We had to integrate sophisticated valuation logic into a batch-processing world without breaking the daily reporting cycle.

03. THE SOLUTION

Unifying the Risk brain and the Trading brain.

My role was to act as the architect of the transition, balancing three competing priorities:

The Quant Alignment (Risk): I ensured "Conceptual Soundness." If the Front Office pricer uses a specific volatility surface, the Risk Model must use the exact same one. I defined the Data Contract—the specific market inputs and trade attributes that ensure the "Risk" price and the "Trade" price are identical.

The Technical Pivot (Systems): We moved toward a Service-Oriented Architecture. By wrapping the C++ pricing libraries into high-performance API calls, we allowed the Legacy Engine to "query" the Front Office’s valuation logic. This wasn't just "making an API"; it was an architectural shift in how risk is calculated.

The Regulatory Defense (Business): I led the Parallel Runs. For months, we sat with the old and new models side-by-side. I had to explain to Model Risk Management (MRM) exactly why the numbers changed. If VaR jumped by 20%, I proved it wasn't a bug—it was the system finally seeing a risk that had been invisible for a decade.

04. THE ASSUMPTIONS

Where it Breaks

The Data "Basis": You can have the best API in the world, but if the Front Office uses "Mid" prices and Risk uses "Bid/Ask," your Full Reval will still fail the PLA test. The struggle is often in the data cleaning, not the code.

The Governance Bottleneck: The hardest part isn't the math—it's the documentation. Convincing a skeptical validator that an API-driven approach is "stable" requires a deep understanding of both the code and the credit risk.

The "Black Box" Trap: When the Risk Model and the Trading Model are the same, you lose your "Second Pair of Eyes." I had to design Independent Sanity Checks (like Greek-based thresholds) to ensure that if the Front Office pricer had a "fat-finger" error, the Risk system would flag it instead of blindly trusting it.

REPLICABLE DIAGNOSTIC

Architecture is the ultimate risk mitigator.

If your risk team spends 80% of their morning explaining away discrepancies between their VaR and the previous day's actual P&L, you don't have a data problem. You have an Architecture Problem.

The move to Full Reval isn't just about passing a regulatory test; it's about making sure that when the next "Black Swan" event hits, the Risk Manager isn't the last person in the room to know the score.

Greeks are an estimate. Full Revaluation is the truth.
